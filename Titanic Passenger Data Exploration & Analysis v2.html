I choose to drop the Embarked variable, as it would have required paired dummy variables to analyze. In our Pearson's R table, we can also observe that FareLoge and Pclass are indeed highly correlated, but this won't be further analyzed. You don't need p-values to tell you that 1st-class tickets cost more.

Our greatest correlation coefficients relative to Survived are FareLoge and Sex. FareLoge, a continuous variable, will be analyzed using a two-sided Student's t-test. Conversely, we will analyze Sex using the Chi-Squared method for significance of expected versus observed values in categorical variables.

Determining whether the relationship between Sex and Survived is statistically significant requires the use of a chi-squared test. The chi-squared test compares observed frequencies within categories against their expected frequencies given no relationship.

In this case, our expected frequency isn't perfectly equal - there are more men aboard the Titanic than women, so we need to calculate the ratio of men to women in order to calculate the expected frequency of survivors.

Because this is our first true statistical test, it may be helpful to be reminded of the limitations of the test. Our chi-squared test will reveal solely whether the relationship revealed by our Pearson's R correlation coefficient is statistically significant. In order to further analyze the extent of the independent variable's influence, we would need to use ANOVA and regression.

For our finding to be rigorous, it must be assumed that the sample data are truly random and representative of the full population of the Titanic. Only then may we assume that the relationship we observe in our sample group may also be applied to the population.

Test parameters as follows:

h0: There is no relationship between Sex and Survived.

h1: Women survive at a higher rate than men.

p = .01

There seems to be a definite difference between the survivors and non-survivors, though there is also quite a bit of overlap. Given that our independent variable is actually the natural log of Fare, this isn't too surprising.

We will now conduct our Student's t-test, a test for statistical significance between categorical and continuous variables. The test determines whether samples within different categories show enough differentiation to be more than random.

Test parameters are as follows:

h0: There is no relationship between FareLoge and Survived.

h1: The mean FareLoge for survivors is higher than for non-survivors.

p = .01
